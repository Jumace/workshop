# Why I build

Curiosity is a superpower if you let yourself follow it.

That sounds simple, but it is easy to forget. Software can become very outcome-driven: tickets, deadlines, releases, frameworks, best practices, and a steady pressure to already know what you are doing. Those things matter, but they can also make tinkering feel harder than it should.

Building helps me understand ideas in a way that reading alone cannot, but it has not always been the easiest first step. Sometimes the distance between an interesting technology and hands-on experience with it was large enough that I would keep it as a mental note instead of making time and energy for a prototype. AI-assisted development changed that balance for me. It makes it easier to move from “I wonder how this works” to “let me try a small version of it” while the curiosity is still fresh.

That renewed energy is not about making the work automatic, and it does not remove the need for judgment, taste, or care. It lowers the friction between curiosity and action. It gives me more room to ask, “What if?” and actually try the thing before the spark disappears.

That matters to me because I do not only want to learn in private. I like enabling others. If I am already exploring a tool, a pattern, or a question, there is a good chance someone else is wondering about a similar thing. Writing it down and sharing the messy middle might help them get started, avoid a dead end, or simply feel less alone in not knowing yet.

<Callout title="What this site is for" tone="note">
  The blog is where I slow down and turn explorations into writing. The lab is where I let
  experiments stay experimental, with clear edges around what works, what is missing, and what I
  still want to understand.
</Callout>

I am intentionally not trying to make this a resume page. I want it to be closer to a workshop: a place for notes, experiments, small discoveries, and the occasional sharper opinion once I have earned it by making something.

The core site should stay portable and simple. The lab can touch specific platforms, AI tools, and other technologies, but those experiments should be honest about their boundaries. If something requires setup, secrets, or a hosted service, I want that to be visible instead of hidden behind a polished demo.
